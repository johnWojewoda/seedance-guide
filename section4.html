04

Multimodal Inputs

This is where the barrier between traditional filmmaking and generative AI completely dissolves. Seedance 2.0 is not just a text-to-video model. It processes text, images, video, and audio all simultaneously — evaluating every reference file you provide to dictate every single frame of your generation.

By using the reference syntax in your prompt, you can upload up to 12 reference files per generation: up to 9 images, 3 videos, and 3 audio files. But uploading assets without giving them explicit jobs in your prompt is like hiring a film crew and letting them do whatever they want on set. The result is chaos — the model will try to mash the visual contents of all your files together.

The critical concept to master here is that you are not uploading pixels. You are uploading logic. You are extracting the physics, the motion vectors, the camera behavior, the audio rhythm — and applying them to your scene.

1

The Reference Syntax

Every reference file you upload gets a numbered tag based on its type. You must call these tags explicitly in your prompt to assign each file a specific job. If you don't call them, the model ignores them or worse — blends them all together.

[Image1] — [Image9] Reference images. Use for character appearance, costume, material textures, first frame pins, and style references.
[Video1] — [Video3] Reference videos. Use for camera movement replication, body motion and blocking, transition rhythm, and style transfer.
[Audio1] — [Audio3] Reference audio files. Use for voice tone matching, ambient mood, and beat timing for synchronized cuts.
Correct syntax — always assign a job

Use [Image1] as the character costume reference. Use [Video1] as the camera movement reference. Use [Audio1] to set the beat timing for scene cuts.

Beginner tip

Never upload a reference file without explicitly calling it in your prompt and assigning it a specific job. An unassigned reference is not neutral — it actively interferes with your generation.

2

Extracting Logic, Not Pixels

This is the most revolutionary concept in Seedance 2.0 and the one most beginners completely miss. When you reference a video, the model does not copy the visual content of that video into your scene. It mathematically isolates the kinetic vectors — the invisible wireframe of how objects move and accelerate — and applies that physics blueprint to your subject.

Think of it this way: imagine a video of a wizard's staff shattering into glowing puzzle pieces that scatter in the wind. When you tell Seedance 2.0 to use that video for transition logic, it ignores the colors, the lighting, and the wizard entirely. It extracts only the movement math — the acceleration curves, the scatter vectors, the timing. You can then apply that exact physics blueprint to a completely different subject.

Logic extraction in practice

Upload a video of a complex shatter effect. In your prompt write: "Use [Video1] for the transition physics only. Apply that movement logic to [Image1] — a ceramic coffee cup on a marble surface."

Beginner tip

You are directing the physics engine using visual data instead of text. The more specific you are about which aspect of the reference to extract — camera movement, body motion, transition rhythm — the more precisely the model will apply it.

3

Image References

Image references are the most commonly used multimodal input. They serve several distinct purposes in Seedance 2.0 — and understanding which job to assign them changes your results dramatically.

Character appearance The most common use. Upload a photo of your subject and reference it as [Image1] in your prompt to anchor their face and appearance throughout the generation.
Costume & material Upload a texture or fabric reference and assign it specifically: "Use [Image2] as the costume texture reference for the character's jacket."
First frame pin Upload an image to use as the exact starting frame of your video. The generation will begin from that composition and animate forward.
Style reference Upload an image with a specific visual style and assign it: "Use [Image3] for the overall color palette and lighting style only."
Beginner tip

The maximum is 9 images but more is not better. Reference hygiene — using fewer, higher quality, specifically assigned references — produces far cleaner results than flooding the model with data. See Section 5 for the full character consistency rules.

4

Video References

Video references are where multimodal input becomes truly powerful. You can upload up to 3 reference videos with a total duration of up to 15 seconds each. The model extracts the motion logic and applies it to your scene — not the visual content.

Camera movement Upload a video with a specific camera move — a complex dolly shot, a drone sweep, a handheld run — and reference it: "Use [Video1] for the camera movement path only."
Body motion & blocking Upload a video of a specific physical performance and transfer that motion to your character: "Use [Video1] as the body movement reference for [Image1]."
Transition rhythm Upload a video with a specific editing rhythm and apply its cut timing to your generation: "Use [Video1] for the transition pacing and cut rhythm only."
Beginner tip

When using a video reference for camera movement, find a video where the camera move is clean and isolated — not mixed with complex subject movement. The cleaner the reference, the more precisely the model can extract the motion logic.

5

Audio References

Audio is no longer just a post-production afterthought. In Seedance 2.0 you can upload up to 3 reference audio files and use them to dictate the mood, beat timing, and even voice tone of your generation — before the first pixel is rendered.

Beat timing Upload a music track and instruct the model to analyze the waveform and cut the scene on the strong beats: "Use [Audio1] to time the scene cuts to the beat."
Ambient mood Upload an ambient soundscape to set the overall audio atmosphere: "Use [Audio1] as the ambient sound reference for the scene."
Voice tone Upload a voice recording to match the tone for dialogue generation: "Use [Audio1] as the voice tone reference for the character's dialogue."
Audio beat sync in practice

[Image1] walking through neon lit city, 35mm anamorphic. Use [Audio1] to sync camera cuts to the beat. When the audio swells at 4 seconds accelerate the push-in to match the emotional weight.

Beginner tip

Audio references ensure the physics engine calculates the rhythm of the scene before it even begins rendering the first pixel. This is one of the most underused features in Seedance 2.0 — most creators don't know it exists.
