Technical Settings

Most people generating AI video with Seedance 2.0 are burning through credits unnecessarily. They generate at high resolution to "see what it looks like", they push clips to 15 seconds because they want more footage, and they never run systematic tests before committing to a final render.

The technical settings are not just sliders and dropdowns. They are decisions that determine whether your physics engine runs clean or collapses into chaos. This section gives you the exact workflow the best creators use to protect their budgets and get consistent results.

1

The 6-8 Second Sweet Spot

Seedance 2.0 technically supports up to 15 seconds per generation. Most people assume longer is better. It is not. Testing across thousands of generations has revealed a hard truth: anything past 12 seconds drastically increases hallucinations.

After the 12 second mark, the model's memory buffer gets overwhelmed. The environment starts drifting. Characters experience mid-stride wardrobe changes. Physics that were stable in the first 8 seconds turn to soup by second 13. The model is simply being forced to remember too many constraints for too long without fresh guidance.

The sweet spot

6-8 seconds per generation. This mimics real filmmaking — an uninterrupted shot in a professional film rarely lasts longer than 8 seconds anyway.

Beginner tip

If you need a longer scene, generate multiple 6-8 second clips and edit them together rather than pushing one generation to 15 seconds. You will get far cleaner results and spend less per usable second of footage.

2

Resolution Strategy — Draft Cheap, Polish Once

This is the single biggest budget mistake beginners make. They generate at 720p or 1080p from the start because they want to see their idea at full quality. The problem is that paying a premium to render at high resolution does not fix an unstable prompt. It just gives you a crisper mistake — a beautifully high-definition version of the uncanny valley.

The correct workflow is to treat 480p as your dress rehearsal. If your kinetic action prompt is vague and the character's walking motion is unstable at 480p, it will be unstable at 1080p too. Fix the wobble while it is cheap. Then pay for the polish once the wireframe behaves correctly.

Resolution cost comparison

480p ~$0.20 · 720p ~$0.50 · 1080p ~$0.90 per 5 second generation

Correct workflow

Draft at 480p → prove the motion is stable → lock the prompt → render once at 1080p

Beginner tip

Never generate at 1080p more than once per prompt. If the result isn't right, go back to 480p, fix the prompt, and test again. The cost difference between getting it wrong at 480p versus 1080p is enormous over time.

3

The 5-Run Sweep

Before you commit to any final render, you need to run a systematic diagnostic. The best creators call this the 5-run sweep — a disciplined workflow of changing one variable at a time to lock in stability before spending on quality.

The trap beginners fall into is changing multiple variables at once. When something goes wrong — and it will — they have no idea which change caused the problem. The 5-run sweep removes that ambiguity entirely.

Step 1 — Duration Test at 6 seconds vs 8 seconds to identify where drift begins. Lock the duration before touching anything else.
Step 2 — Aspect ratio Confirm the composition holds in your chosen ratio. Lock it.
Step 3 — Motion stability Verify the core kinetic action is stable at 480p. If not, refine the prompt and retest.
Step 4 — Lighting & style Confirm the lighting holds consistently across the full duration. Lock it.
Step 5 — Final render Only now bump to 720p or 1080p. Everything is locked. This is your one paid render.
Beginner tip

Run every sweep step at 480p. Do not move to the next step until the current one is stable. This discipline will save you significant credits every single session.

4

Audio Settings

Seedance 2.0 generates synchronized audio by default — including background music, sound effects, and ambient noise. Most beginners leave this on and accept whatever the model produces. Professional creators treat audio as a deliberate technical decision, not an afterthought.

generate_audio: true Default setting. Seedance 2.0 will attempt to generate synchronized audio. Guide it explicitly in your prompt — "ambient birdsong only" or "soft wind sound, no music".
generate_audio: false Disables audio generation entirely. Use this when you plan to add your own audio in post-production or when you want a completely silent base render.
Beginner tip

If you leave audio on but don't guide it in your prompt, Seedance 2.0 will invent whatever it thinks fits the scene — usually generic background music you don't want. Either turn it off or be very explicit: "soft wind rustling leaves, no music, no soundtrack".

5

Seed Locking

Every generation in Seedance 2.0 uses a random seed by default — a number that initializes the physics engine's random state. This means two identical prompts will produce two different videos. Once you find a generation you like, locking the seed lets you make minor iterations without losing the core look you achieved.

How to use it

When a generation looks right, note the seed number from the output. Set that seed in your next generation to use the same random initialization — then make small prompt adjustments to refine without starting from scratch.

Beginner tip

Seed locking is most powerful for character consistency across multiple shots. Once you find a seed that renders your character correctly, lock it and use it for every shot in that scene.

Complete Technical Settings workflow

Step 1: Draft at 480p, 6 seconds, 16:9. Step 2: Confirm motion is stable. Step 3: Lock duration and ratio. Step 4: Confirm lighting holds. Step 5: Render once at 1080p with generate_audio: false. Add your own audio in post.
