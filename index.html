The Five-Part Spine

Most people typing prompts into Seedance 2.0 are essentially gambling. They write a sentence, hit generate, spend $0.90, and hope for the best. That's the old way.

The Five-Part Spine is the prompting architecture that separates creators who get consistent, professional results from those who waste credits chasing lucky generations.

Every prompt you write in Seedance 2.0 should have these five parts — in this order:

1

Subject Anchor

This is who or what your video is about. In Seedance 2.0, if you upload a reference image of a person or character, you refer to them as [Image1] in your prompt. This tells Seedance 2.0 to use that specific face and appearance throughout the entire video — not a random person it invents.

Example

[Image1] standing at the edge of a rooftop...

Beginner tip

If you don't upload a reference image yet, just describe your subject in detail instead. "A tall woman with short silver hair wearing a black leather jacket" works fine to start.

2

Kinetic Action

This is what your subject is doing — and it needs to be specific. Seedance 2.0 responds much better to precise physical descriptions than vague ones. Vague actions produce wobbly, uncertain motion. Specific actions produce controlled, intentional movement.

Example

slow deliberate walking gait, coat edge catching wind

Beginner tip

Avoid just saying "walking" or "standing". Add adverbs and physical detail — "slow deliberate walking gait" gives Seedance 2.0 much more to work with.

3

Camera Logic

This is how Seedance 2.0 "sees" the scene. Instead of saying "cinematic" — which means nothing to the model — use real lens language that filmmakers use. Specifying a lens type fundamentally changes how Seedance 2.0 constructs depth, focus, and light in your video.

Example

35mm anamorphic lens, slow push-in

Beginner tip

35mm anamorphic = wide, dramatic, movie-like. 85mm prime = tight, intimate, portrait-style. Start with these two until you're comfortable.

4

Style & Lighting

Lighting is the single highest-impact variable in Seedance 2.0. A well-lit prompt can elevate an average scene into something that looks broadcast-ready. The key is to describe the physics of the light — not just the mood.

Example

volumetric rim lighting from behind, soft blue ambient fill

Beginner tip

Instead of "dramatic lighting" try "hard rim light at 45 degrees casting long shadows". Instead of "golden hour" try "warm low-angle sunlight with long shadows and lens flare".

5

Shot Transitions

Seedance 2.0 can generate up to 15 seconds of video in a single generation. That's enough for multiple shots — but only if you tell it exactly where to cut. Use bracketed commands to direct Seedance 2.0 like a real film director giving instructions to a crew.

Example

[Shot 1: Wide] [Image1] on rooftop at night. [Cut to: Close-up] face lit by city glow below.

Beginner tip

For your first attempts, stick to one shot. Once you're comfortable with the Five-Part Spine, add a second shot using [Cut to:] and see what happens.

Complete Five-Part Spine prompt

[Image1] slow deliberate walking gait coat edge catching wind, 35mm anamorphic lens slow push-in, volumetric rim lighting from behind soft blue ambient fill, [Cut to: Close-up] face reflected in rain-soaked glass
