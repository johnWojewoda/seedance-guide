The Five-Part Spine

Most people typing prompts into Seedance 2.0 are essentially gambling. They write a sentence, hit generate, spend $0.90, and hope for the best. That's the old way.

The Five-Part Spine is the prompting architecture that separates creators who get consistent, professional results from those who waste credits chasing lucky generations.

Here is something most people don't know: Seedance 2.0 places its heaviest mathematical weight on the first 20 to 30 words of your prompt. If your subject isn't pinned down immediately, the physics engine has no gravity — it doesn't know what object to apply its calculations to, and it starts hallucinating new characters to fill the void.

Every prompt you write in Seedance 2.0 should have these five parts — in this order:

1

Subject Anchor

This is who or what your video is about — and it must come first. Seedance 2.0 is built on a real world physics simulation engine. It needs to know immediately what object to apply its calculations to. If you start with the environment or the mood, the engine has no gravity and will invent characters to fill the void.

If you upload a reference image of a person or character, refer to them as [Image1] in your prompt. This tells Seedance 2.0 to use that specific appearance throughout the entire video.

Example

[Image1] standing at the edge of a rooftop...

Without a reference image

A tall woman with short silver hair wearing a black leather jacket, standing at the edge of a rooftop...

Beginner tip

If you start your prompt by describing the rain or the gloomy lighting before your subject, the AI is already lost. It will try to apply the physics of a human to the rain itself. Subject first — always.

2

Kinetic Action

This is what your subject is doing — and Seedance 2.0 does not respond to mood. It responds to physics. The engine needs hard physical verbs it can calculate vectors from. Soft transformation words give it nothing to simulate.

Hard verbs — use these

drags, fractures, implodes, stretches, collapses, jerks, pivots, slams

Soft words — avoid these

"becomes", "turns into", "transforms", "feels like", "seems to"

Example

slow deliberate walking gait, coat edge catching wind

Beginner tip

If you want a glass to shatter, don't write "the glass becomes dust" — the AI will just cross-fade the pixels like a cheap PowerPoint transition. Write "the glass fractures" or "the glass implodes". Hard verbs give the engine the vectors it needs to simulate actual movement.

3

Camera Logic

This is how Seedance 2.0 sees the scene. The word "cinematic" means nothing to the model. What does work is real lens language — the same language a director uses on a film set. Specifying a lens type fundamentally changes how Seedance 2.0 constructs depth, focus, and light in your video.

Example

35mm anamorphic lens, slow push-in

Beginner tip

35mm anamorphic = wide, dramatic, movie-like. 85mm prime = tight, intimate, portrait-style. Always combine a lens type with a movement — "85mm prime lens, slow push-in" gives Seedance 2.0 far more to work with than either one alone.

4

Style & Lighting

Lighting is the single highest-impact variable in Seedance 2.0. A well-lit prompt can elevate an average scene into something that looks broadcast-ready. The key is to describe the physics of the light — not just the mood. Only after you have locked the subject, the movement, and the camera do you introduce your style and lighting flavor.

Example

volumetric rim lighting from behind, soft blue ambient fill

Beginner tip

Instead of "dramatic lighting" try "hard rim light at 45 degrees casting long shadows". Instead of "golden hour" try "warm low-angle sunlight with long shadows and lens flare". Describe the physics of the light, not the feeling it creates.

5

Shot Transitions

Seedance 2.0 can generate up to 15 seconds of video in a single generation — enough for multiple shots. But if you don't explicitly command the cuts, the AI will try to cram every action you described into the first two seconds. The result is hyper-speed, unnatural, jerky movement. Use bracketed commands to schedule exactly when each shot happens.

Basic cut

[Shot 1: Wide] [Image1] on rooftop at night. [Cut to: Close-up] face lit by city glow below.

Timeline method

0-4 seconds: slow push-in on subject. 4-8 seconds: camera orbits to profile view.

Beginner tip

For your first attempts stick to one shot. Once you are comfortable with the Five-Part Spine, try the timeline method — give the engine a mechanical schedule of events with exact timestamps. It removes the AI's permission to guess.

Complete Five-Part Spine prompt

[Image1] slow deliberate walking gait coat edge catching wind, 35mm anamorphic lens slow push-in, volumetric rim lighting from behind soft blue ambient fill, [Cut to: Close-up] face reflected in rain-soaked glass
