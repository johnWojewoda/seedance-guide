Dialogue & Audio

Most creators treat audio as something you add in post-production — slap a music track on in Premiere Pro and call it done. In Seedance 2.0, that approach leaves one of the most powerful features completely unused.

Sound in Seedance 2.0 is not a decoration. It is a physics input. You can instruct the model to analyze an audio waveform and literally cut the scene on the strong beats of a track. You can synchronize camera acceleration to an audio swell. You can match voice tone for dialogue generation. All of this happens before the first pixel is rendered.

And when it comes to dialogue — getting characters to speak naturally within Seedance 2.0's time constraints requires specific techniques that most creators never discover. This section covers all of it.

1

How Audio Works in Seedance 2.0

When you enable audio generation and provide guidance in your prompt, Seedance 2.0 analyzes the audio instructions before it begins rendering. The physics engine uses the audio rhythm to calculate the pacing of the entire scene — when to cut, when to accelerate the camera, when to hold on a face.

This means audio is not added after the video is generated. It shapes the video as it is being generated. A scene with a fast beat reference will produce tighter, more rhythmic cuts. A scene with a slow ambient reference will produce longer, more contemplative movements.

generate_audio: true Audio generation is on by default. Always guide it explicitly in your prompt or Seedance 2.0 will invent whatever it thinks fits — usually generic background music you don't want.
generate_audio: false Disables audio entirely. Use this when you plan to add your own audio in post-production. Gives you a clean silent base render.
Beginner tip

Never leave audio on without guiding it. "Soft wind rustling leaves, no music, no soundtrack" is far better than letting the model choose. An unguided audio generation almost always produces something that fights your visuals.

2

Audio Prompt Syntax

Guiding audio in your text prompt follows the same physics-first principles as everything else in Seedance 2.0. Describe what the audio physically is — not the mood it creates. Specific physical descriptions produce specific audio. Vague mood descriptions produce generic noise.

Specific — use this approach

soft wind rustling dry leaves, distant water flowing over stones, no music, no soundtrack

Vague — avoid this

peaceful ambient sound, calm atmosphere

Explicit exclusions

no music, no soundtrack, no birdsong, complete silence except for soft wind

Beginner tip

Seedance 2.0 defaults strongly to birdsong and generic background music. If you want neither, you must explicitly exclude both. "No music, no birdsong, soft wind only" is the minimum exclusion set for a clean natural ambient result.

3

Beat Synchronization

This is one of the most underused and most powerful features in Seedance 2.0. By uploading an audio reference file and instructing the model to sync to its waveform, you can make the physics engine calculate the entire rhythm of your scene before rendering begins. Camera cuts happen on the beat. Camera acceleration matches audio swells. The result is video that feels professionally edited — because it was, before a single frame was generated.

[Image1] walking through neon-lit city, 35mm anamorphic. [Shot 1: Wide] 0-4 seconds slow push-in. [Cut to: Close-up] 4-8 seconds. Use [Audio1] to sync all cuts to the beat. Camera acceleration matches audio swell at 4 seconds.

Beginner tip

For beat sync to work well, upload a clean audio reference with a clear, consistent beat — not a complex orchestral piece where the beat is buried. A simple drum track or a song with a strong, predictable rhythm gives the model the clearest waveform to analyze.

4

Generating Dialogue

Getting characters to speak naturally in Seedance 2.0 is one of the hardest things to do well. The model wants to rush. Within a tight 6-8 second generation, characters will try to cram all their dialogue into the first few seconds — talking over each other, skipping natural pauses, producing robotic unnatural delivery.

The solution is pause injection — a simple but highly specific technique discovered by JS FilmZ. You literally type pause commands directly into your dialogue block using parentheses. This acts as a hard stop command for the physics engine, physically freezing the character's mouth movements and forcing natural pacing.

Pause injection syntax

Character says: "I have been waiting for you." (1 second pause) "I knew you would come."

Full dialogue prompt with pause injection

[Image1] standing in doorway, 85mm prime, soft rim light. Character says: "You should not have come here." (1 second pause) Looks away. (0.5 second pause) "But I am glad you did."

Beginner tip

You are not directing a human actor who naturally pauses for dramatic effect. You are programming a system. You have to write the code for the silence. Every pause must be explicitly typed — the model will not add them on its own.

5

Dialogue with Audio References

For the most realistic dialogue generation, combine pause injection with an audio reference for voice tone. Upload a short audio clip of the voice you want to match — even a 5-10 second sample — and reference it in your prompt. The model will use the tonal qualities of that voice to shape the character's speech.

Voice tone reference syntax

Use [Audio1] as the voice tone reference for [Image1]'s dialogue. Character says: "The city never sleeps." (1 second pause) "Neither do I."

Voice tone Upload a voice recording to match the character's speech pattern, accent, and tonal quality.
Lip sync Upload a reference audio of the exact dialogue you want spoken. The model will attempt to match mouth movements to the audio waveform.
Beginner tip

For lip sync to work accurately, the audio reference must be clean with no background music or noise. A dry vocal recording with no reverb gives the model the clearest waveform to map mouth movements to.

Complete Dialogue & Audio prompt

[Image1] standing in neon-lit doorway, 85mm prime slow push-in. Use [Audio1] as voice tone reference. Character says: "You should not have come here." (1 second pause) Looks away slowly. (0.5 second pause) "But I am glad you did." Soft rain ambience only, no music, no soundtrack.
